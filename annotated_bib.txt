-- SUMMARY PAPERS -- 
# Monitoring the Ocean Environment Using Robotic Systems: Advancements, Trends, and Challenges
## Nitin Agarwala
## 2026
Summary paper discussing the current trends of marine robotic systems. The
article stems from the fact that marine applications and data collecting
are becoming more relevant in recent years. It covers
classifications of different marine vehicles and their main purposes. It states
that the majority of progress in marine robotics is being done for military purposes,
but recent years have shown that research and scientific purposes for marine
robots have been on an uptick. The main challenges it proposes are communication methods,
powering robots, and sensor demands.

# Robots for ecological monitoring: a review
## Patrick White et al.
## 2026
Another summary paper discussing the different methods and potential impacts
of using robotic systems to monitor ecological data. This article mainly focuses
on marine robotics since it is the most common form of ecological robotic system, but
also includes some aerial and terrestrial robotic systems. The paper, similar to the
previous one, cites the need for robotic systems to monitor the environment as they
are better equipped for long term projects. Additionally, due to changes in the
environment like climate change being a thing, humans need to study the environment
more carefully and consistently than ever. The article states that there are huge
advancements in the technology of robots in ecology, but there is limited
information and study being done on the disturbance generated by the robots
in their target environment.

The paper also notes that the disparity between marine and other forms of robotic
ecological systems is that the ocean proves more difficult and unpredictable
for humans to interact with, thus increasing the desire to find technological
solutions. Additionally, within marine robotics, it finds freshwater systems
less common than oceanic systems.

# Technologies for marine biodiversity monitoring and mapping: A systemic review
## Ucciero et al.
## 2026
Yet another summary paper. This one confers with the two previous. Notable insights
for their results are that threats to marine biodiversity are rarely considered, many
studies involve proprietary software or non-commercial tools, and do not explicitly
mention the cost of implementation. The paper also notes a trend of most
robotic systems involving some form of AI, usually for data analysis. Also discussed
is the possibility of universal standards for ocean data gathering to benefit
marine science as a whole.

-- ROBOT-ANIMAL INTERACTION --
# Animal-robot interaction--an emerging field at the intersection of biology
and robotics
## Romano et al.
## 2024
General summary paper showing that there is an increase in the interest
of robot-fish interaction studies. All studies mentioned were biomemetic, not
many studies are being done on just general disturbance of a normal robot.

# Ecology of fear in highly invasive fish revealed by robots
## Polverino et al.
## 2021
A fish inspired robot was used to directly affect the fish that it
was monitoring. 
The robot mimicked a predator for mosquitofish. The study found the presence
of the robot increased stress responses from mosquitofish and made them less
likely to predate on native tadpoles.
An example of robots being used to measure ecological
response to specific stimuli, but still not passive disturbance measurements.

# How low can you go? Exploring impact of drones on haul out behaviour of
harbour - and grey seals
## Stepien et al.
## 2024
Uses drones at varying heights to determine the change of behaviour in
seals. This shows a direct relationship between a non-biomemetic robot
platform and the ecosystem it is involved in. The study found that
there is a threshold in which seals were disturbed by the drones. However,
there are many other environmental factors at play that change how the seals
behaved while a drone was descending. Above anything else, it just shows
that you got to be careful when interacting with wildlife. Also that
measuring interactions is incredibly species dependent.

# Salmon behavioural response to robots in an aquaculture sea cage
## Kruusmaa et al.
## 2020
Specifically a fish interaction study with a non-biomemetic robot. This
one also includes a reproducible way to analyze video of fish
to determine behavior. The study uses tailbeat frequencies to measure
fish response. The article finds several conclusions. One, fish
have a higher avoidance reaction when a diver was present with the
robot. Two, locomotion possibly had no effect on fish avoidance - where
it is also likely that size of the vessel contributes to avoidance. Third,
color of the vessel had little effect. Some cues for humans that make
sense effect animals differently.

-- MEASURING AND DOCUMENTING FISH --
# Review of Methods for Sampling Fish in Structured Habitats
## Bacheler
## 2025
A summary article about the different ways to document fish. In this case,
a structured habitat is anything that has a lot of alcoves or places for
fish to hide. Estuaries are mentioned as a structured environment but only
briefly. The main portion we are interested in is the visual approaches as
we will be using SONAR or a camera to detect fish. It notes that ROVs are
better at fish surveying than other video based methods, with the main
drawback being how fish interact with a tool that makes noise. 

For acoustic gear like SONAR, they are generally better for more turbid
water as they do not need to account for clarity like video.

The article also talks about ASVs. Their main drawbacks are that data
collected has to be validated as it is collected autonomously. However,
they prove better at not disturbing fish than typical research vessels.
Additionally, they are generally cheaper and easier to maintain the
fully crewed vessels.

Part of the article's call to action is to look into more novel ways
of fish surveying, like ASVs.

The best way to sample fish really depends on the species and environment.
Go figure.

# Fish avoidance of research vessels and the efficacy of noise-reduced
vessels: a review
## Robertis
## 2012
An analysis of fish behavior when a research vessel is approaching them
and a dive (ha ha) into what exactly causes a reaction. Sound or
possibly something else. The reaction fish give to approaching vessels
is somewhat inconsistent, with some fish moving towards and some moving
away from its approach. However, it does show that there is at least
some reaction happening to a typical research vessel.

Fish reaction impacts the measurements taken of the fish negatively, producing
inaccurate results.

Studies examined found that with pollock, a noise reduced vessel resulted
in decreased avoidance behavior. However, work done on herring suggests
that this isn't always the case. Another example of it really depends
on the environment and the fish - they all react differently to
different stimuli.

It is possible that fish react not just to the sound itself, but the
"suddenness" of the sound. Also different fish respond to different
frequencies differently.
